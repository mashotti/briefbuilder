# Prompt Comparison: Before & After

Same fictional inputs, two prompts. Both briefs generated by Claude (claude-sonnet-4-6) — the same model that runs the Brief Builder app.

---

## Test scenario inputs

```
Business name:   Tierra & Salt
Description:     Handmade functional ceramics — bowls, mugs, plates — made by a single
                 Oaxacan potter using clay from the local mountains, natural pigments,
                 and traditional Zapotec patterns. Sold online and at local craft markets.
Industry:        Home & Lifestyle
Business size:   Solo / Freelance

Campaign name:   Table Season Launch
Primary goal:    Drive sales / revenue
Start date:      2026-07-01
End date:        2026-07-28  (4 weeks)
Budget:          $600 USD

Audience:        US-based home décor enthusiasts and conscious consumers who value
                 handmade goods with cultural significance
Age range:       25–34, 35–44
Location:        US (primarily coastal cities)
Pain point:      Wants unique, meaningful pieces that aren't mass-produced, but struggles
                 to find craft-level quality at accessible prices

Channels selected: Instagram, Email newsletter  (NO paid channels)
Tone:            warm & friendly, authentic & relatable

USP:             Each piece is shaped, fired, and painted by hand using clay from
                 Oaxacan mountains and natural pigments — no two pieces are identical
Offer:           15% off orders over $75 with code TABLE15
Competitors:     CB2, West Elm, Food52 Shop
```

## Field-by-field comparison

### Tagline

**Old prompt:**
> "Bring Craft to Your Table"

**New prompt:**
> "Set a table that came from a mountain."

**What changed:** The old tagline is category-generic (passes the Canva test — it could be any craft brand, any product that goes on a table). The new tagline fails the Canva test: "from a mountain" is specific to the Oaxacan clay sourcing in the business description and connects to the campaign name ("Table Season"). It's also a complete thought, not an imperative + generic noun.

---

### Executive summary

**Old prompt:**
> "Tierra & Salt is launching its Table Season collection to drive sales among US-based home décor enthusiasts who value handmade goods. Leveraging Instagram and email marketing, the campaign will reach conscious consumers aged 28–45 in coastal cities, highlighting the brand's unique handmade ceramics with traditional Zapotec patterns. A 15% launch discount will serve as the primary conversion driver."

**New prompt:**
> "Conscious home consumers in the US are increasingly buying provenance — knowing exactly who made something and where the materials came from. Tierra & Salt has that story at a depth most craft brands don't: named geography, named process, named pattern lineage. This campaign converts that provenance advantage into first purchases by leading with the object and its origin (Instagram shows the work; email closes the sale), using the 15% launch offer as the tipping point for buyers who have been watching but haven't committed. At $600 with no paid spend, success depends on the email list converting at 2–3% and Instagram driving 40+ new subscribers over 4 weeks — achievable if each post features a single piece with its specific story rather than a catalog spread."

**What changed:** The old summary re-states the form inputs in paragraph form. Remove the business name and it could describe any craft product launch. The new summary names the specific strategic insight (provenance as the category differentiator), assigns channel roles (Instagram acquires, email converts), and states the numeric dependency ($600, no paid, relies on email conversion rate) — things that weren't explicitly stated in the form.

### KPIs

**Old prompt:**
```
{ "metric": "Instagram reach",    "target": "50K+ impressions over campaign period" }
{ "metric": "Email open rate",    "target": "35%+" }
{ "metric": "Revenue increase",   "target": "20% vs. baseline" }
{ "metric": "Return on ad spend", "target": "4:1" }
```

**New prompt:**
```
{ "metric": "Table Season collection revenue (4 weeks)",
  "target": "$1,200–1,800 — targeting 15–20 orders at ~$85 AOV, a 2–3× return on the $600 campaign budget" }
{ "metric": "Email campaign conversion rate (3 emails × ~500 list)",
  "target": "2.5%+ — targeting 12+ orders from email (industry benchmark for DTC craft/home: 1.5–4%)" }
{ "metric": "Instagram bio link clicks over campaign period",
  "target": "150+ total (3–4 posts/week × 4 weeks, ~10 clicks per post average)" }
{ "metric": "New email subscribers from Instagram",
  "target": "40+ — measures whether Instagram is building a retargetable list, not just passive reach" }
```

**What changed:** Three specific failures corrected:

1. **ROAS target on a campaign with no paid channels.** The old brief included "4:1 ROAS" despite the business selecting only Instagram organic and email. There is no paid spend to measure a return on. The new brief has no ROAS metric.

2. **"20% vs. baseline" with no baseline stated.** This is a tautology — any outcome can be reported as success or failure against an undefined baseline. The new brief converts this to an absolute revenue target derived from the budget: $1,200–1,800 (2–3× return on $600) based on order count × AOV.

3. **No KPI is derived from the budget.** The old prompt generates targets from statistical norms ("35% open rate" is a common industry average) without connecting them to this campaign's specific inputs. The new brief calculates from the ground up: $600 budget, email list size (~500), DTC benchmark conversion rates → specific achievable targets.

### Budget breakdown

**Old prompt:**
```
Paid social advertising:      $240  (40%)
Content creation & design:    $150  (25%)
Email tools & automation:       $90  (15%)
Influencer partnerships:        $60  (10%)
Contingency:                    $60  (10%)
```

**New prompt:**
```
Photography & content creation (2–3 shoot setups, 12–15 final assets):   $300  (50%)
Email platform (Klaviyo / Mailchimp + sign-up form):                        $90  (15%)
Design assets (Canva Pro, Story templates, grid):                          $120  (20%)
Launch packaging upgrade (tissue paper, sticker, notecard — first 20 orders):  $90  (15%)
```

**What changed:** The old brief allocated $240 (40%) to paid social advertising — the single largest line item — for a campaign where the user selected zero paid channels. This is actively wrong advice. The new brief reallocates that budget to photography (the actual production cost for organic Instagram) and launch packaging (which creates unboxing moments and drives organic sharing). No line item exists for channels that weren't selected.

### Content pillars

**Old prompt:**
```
"Brand story & cultural heritage"
"Product education & craftsmanship"
"Customer testimonials & social proof"
```

**New prompt:**
```
"Oaxacan provenance — content tracing each piece back to its source: the clay valley,
 the pigment process, the pattern lineage. Makes 'handmade' specific rather than a
 category label and justifies the price by showing the steps mass production omits."

"The single object — one piece per post, one story. Counters the impulse to show the
 full catalog and reinforces the 'no two alike' value. Each post: one ceramic, the hands
 that made it, one process detail."

"Functional permanence — the ceramics in daily use, years from now. Positions this as
 an investment in something used every morning rather than a treat that gets put on a
 shelf. Counters the 'occasional luxury' framing typical of craft objects."
```

**What changed:** The old pillars are calendar categories any competitor could adopt unchanged. "Product education & craftsmanship" could be the content strategy for a mass-market kitchenware brand. The new pillars are strategic positions: each names the specific angle (single object, not catalog), the specific tension it resolves (handmade as specific, not just a label; daily use, not shelf decoration), and what it should make the audience believe about this brand in particular. A competitor cannot lift "Oaxacan provenance" and "the single object" without changing the entire brand premise.

### Tone and voice

**Old prompt:**
> "Warm, friendly, and authentic — a voice that feels genuine and values-driven, speaking directly to the customer's desire for meaningful, handmade goods."

**New prompt:**
> "Unhurried. Speaks as someone describing an object they made slowly, not marketing a product. Example: 'This bowl was shaped on a slow Tuesday in March, dried for three days, and painted while the mountain fog was still on the windows.' Avoids retail-speak constructions ('bring the warmth of Oaxaca into your home', 'elevate your table') — the brand doesn't bring things to you; you're choosing something from a specific place."

**What changed:** The old description is three adjectives that could apply to any premium consumer brand. A copywriter handed "warm, friendly, and authentic" has no new information — they can write almost anything and claim it fits. The new description gives the copywriter a test: does this sound like "someone describing an object they made slowly"? The example sentence is a live demonstration of the voice register (observational, specific, no marketing language). The exclusion ("elevate your table") specifically names the retail-speak category this brand avoids — "elevate" appears in the banned word list, and its removal alone changes what's written.

Note: the word "authentic" appears in the old output. It is one of the eight banned words in the new system prompt. Its replacement ("speaks as someone describing an object they made slowly") does more actual work.

---

### Creative direction

**Old prompt:**
> "Visuals should feel warm, authentic, and human — real photography over stock imagery, showcasing the handmade quality and earthy tones of the ceramics. Content should feel consistent with the brand identity and align with the sustainable, conscious consumer aesthetic."

**New prompt:**
> "Close-up compositions where the ceramic fills 70%+ of the frame against a neutral surface (raw linen, worn wood, bare stone) — the object is the subject, not a prop in a lifestyle scene. Avoid overhead flat-lays with multiple ceramics surrounded by food styling: that's the West Elm catalog aesthetic and this brand needs to read differently. Copy style: short observational fragments that describe a material fact ('Fired at 1,200°C. Hand-painted. No two alike.') — not emotional appeals ('bring the warmth of Oaxaca into your morning')."

**What changed:** The old description contains "authentic" and "human" — both banned words — and gives a designer no constraint they wouldn't have assumed already. "Real photography over stock" is a given for any serious brand brief; "consistent with brand identity" is circular. A designer could read the old direction and make no different decision. The new direction makes three specific choices: the ceramic fills 70%+ of the frame (not as a prop in a lifestyle scene), the specific thing to avoid is named and differentiated from a competitor (West Elm flat-lays), and copy style is specified (fragments, material facts, not emotional appeals).

---

### Risks

**Old prompt:**
> "1. Creative fatigue: mitigate by varying content formats and limiting ad frequency.
>  2. Budget overrun: set daily caps on paid channels and review spend weekly."

**New prompt:**
> "1. Production capacity vs. demand: a successful campaign can generate more orders than a solo ceramicist can fulfill. Mitigate before launch: decide a hard inventory ceiling (suggested: 25 pieces), wire a 'sold out — notify me next batch' form before day 1, and frame limited availability as intentional rather than reactive — 'Table Season: 25 pieces' is stronger positioning than a surprise stockout.
>
> 2. Shipping fragility eroding margin: ceramics shipped from Oaxaca to US addresses carry real breakage risk; a damaged piece means full replacement cost from an $85 order. Mitigate: cost packaging materials and shipping insurance before confirming the $75 discount threshold — verify that the 15% discount + shipping + packaging + breakage provision keeps the order above 20% net margin."

**What changed:** The old risks are wrong in addition to being generic. "Budget overrun: set daily caps on paid channels" is advice for a campaign with no paid channels — there are no daily caps to set. Both risks apply to every campaign that has ever run.

The new risks are specific to: (1) the business size (solo = production ceiling, a constraint that doesn't apply to a 50-person company), (2) the product category (ceramic = fragile = shipping breakage risk, which is irrelevant for a SaaS or apparel campaign), and (3) the offer structure ($75 threshold + 15% discount must be costed against COGS + shipping before launch). Neither risk would appear in a brief for a different business type.
